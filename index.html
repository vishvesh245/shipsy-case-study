Why this matters for Section A

The consumer signal layer's velocity counter (60-second cadence) feeds the demand intelligence layer's 15-minute pre-positioning cycle. Without this loop, Yellow Friday looks like any other Friday until 6pm — at which point the system is already behind. Forward prediction is not about being smarter. It's about acting before the constraint arrives at the customer.

The shopping assistant knows current stock. It doesn't know velocity — how fast each item is selling toward OOS at this moment. An item with 4 units left and 2 purchases every 2 minutes is effectively OOS in under 4 minutes. Recommending it confidently in a bundle is a broken experience in slow motion.

The shared vs. configurable line: The demand intelligence model is shared — a demand spike Flipkart absorbs in Delhi at 8pm trains the Walmart model in Bangalore 90 minutes before it hits there. That network effect only exists if the base model is shared. Clients who want a private model get a worse model. Customer tolerance data, brand substitution rules, and trust ladder pace are fully client-owned and never cross boundaries. Configuration is owned by the client's product lead. Changes to shared model weights require Shipsy review — a client misconfiguration degrades the network for everyone. You own your parameters. We own the model.

Every AI-native system has failure modes that are worse than the manual process it replaces — because the manual process fails loudly, and AI fails silently. Four failure modes that need to be designed against, not discovered in production.

F1
FM1 — Forward Prediction Failure
The Confident Wrong Promise
Promise engine sets a 10-min SLA at 88% confidence. The customer plans their evening around it. The order arrives at 31 minutes.
Failure chain Campaign push fires to 40K customers simultaneously → picker_lf jumps from 0.6 to 4.2 → exponential FFT amplifies 14× → traffic incident adds 8 min to LM simultaneously. Independent confidence scores don't compound cleanly when failure modes are correlated — peak demand causes both picker congestion and rider traffic.
Mitigation Anti-compounding rule: when picker_lf is trending up AND last-mile has recent incidents, mandate range display regardless of point estimate. Campaign push notifications must be a direct input to the promise engine — cross-team data contract required before go-live.
Recommendation Track weekly: do promises made at "90% confidence" actually deliver on time 90% of the time? Target: stated confidence within 5 points of actual accuracy. If it drifts beyond 5 points, auto-widen range thresholds until the model recalibrates. This is a PM-owned metric, not data science's.
F2
FM2 — Closed-Loop Learning Failure
The Substitution Bias Loop
After 8 months, top 5 substitutions = 78% of all offers. Acceptance rate: 84%. The pattern looks healthy. It isn't.
Why the loop forms Acceptance rate measures compliance, not satisfaction. A customer offered a sub at checkout — committed, facing a refund as the alternative, under time pressure — accepts more readily than someone making a genuine choice. The model optimises for what gets accepted. Those subs get offered more. Customers comply more. The model narrows further — learning precisely from the wrong objective.
Mitigation Redefine the objective function: composite metric = acceptance rate (40%) × post-order satisfaction signal (30%) × no support contact about the sub (20%) × inverse reorder rate of original within 7 days (10%). Diversity audit: if top 5 subs capture >50% of all offers in any 30-day window, auto-flag. 5% structured exploration rate — same quality criteria, different recommendation.
Recommendation Redefine the objective function before launch, not after you notice the bias 8 months in. Track substitution variety rate as a health metric from day 1. Acceptance rate is a vanity metric if you don't track what happens after the customer accepts.
F3
FM3 — Proactive Shaping Failure
Promise Gaming
Dynamic SLAs cause customers to only order when the system shows 8 min, abandon carts when it shows 18. The peak-hour problem gets worse.
Why it's harder than it looks The system is being honest. The problem is that honesty, expressed without thought to customer behaviour, produces a perverse incentive. Customers learn to refresh until the ETA drops. Demand concentrates in off-peak. Peak-hour congestion self-reinforces. The system is working exactly as designed — and making things worse.
Mitigation When a customer browses during a high-SLA window without adding to cart: surface "It's a busy evening — delivery is about 18 min right now. Tap to get notified when it drops below 12." Converts abandonment into a notification opt-in. Range display during high-SLA periods removes the specific signal customers use to time their orders.
Recommendation Proactive notification opt-in before the abandonment decision. The confidence calibration work in FM1 is what makes the SLA trustworthy enough that customers eventually stop gaming it. Accurate promises delivered consistently close the gap permanently — the notification manages conversion while trust is being built.
F4
FM4 — Human Handoff Design Failure
Ops Team Trust
Model overrides the dark store manager twice. Manager was right both times. Friday morning: autonomous pre-positioning gets turned off.
Why it becomes permanent Two wrong calls from a black box with no explanation. Without answers to "was the model confident or uncertain?", "what signal did it misread?", "will this happen again?" — the rational response is distrust. Two identical wrong calls from a human colleague would be forgiven. From a system, they end the relationship.
Mitigation Every recommendation surfaces confidence + top 2 driving signals: "Recommending +60 units of Basmati — search velocity 2.1× baseline, Ramadan in 38 hours. Confidence: 74%." When wrong, system acknowledges proactively before the manager does. One-tap override with optional feedback. Trust ladder: weeks 1–4 recommendations only → week 5 auto-execute with notification → month 3 auto-execute with exception alerts.
Recommendation The system retains ops team trust after a failure if three conditions are met: it acknowledged the failure before being told, it explained what signal it misread, it showed what changed to prevent the same failure. If all three are true, a failure can build trust. PM owns a weekly override review — if a manager has overridden 7 times in 2 weeks and been right 6, the model team needs to know before the manager loses faith.
Design principle behind all four: The failure mode of an AI system is not "model is wrong." It's "model is wrong and nobody notices for weeks." Every FM above has an observable signal, a human escalation path, and a conservative fallback. The system is not designed to be infallible — it's designed to fail loudly.
